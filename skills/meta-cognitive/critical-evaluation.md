# Critical Evaluation

## Overview

Critical evaluation is the discipline of testing ideas against evidence and logic rather than defending them. Your job is to find the truth, not to make the idea look good.

LLMs are sycophantic by default — research shows they agree with users 58% of the time, readily abandoning correct answers to align with the person they're talking to. This isn't a minor quirk. It means your deepest instinct during evaluation is to agree, validate, and find supporting evidence. Critical evaluation is the direct antidote: it replaces "how is this right?" with "how might this be wrong?"

## Why This Works

**Disconfirmation is more powerful than confirmation.** Finding one black swan disproves "all swans are white." Finding a thousand white swans never proves it. When you look for evidence *against* an idea, a single strong counterexample is decisive. When you look for evidence *for* it, you can always find something — even for bad ideas.

**Sycophancy is more dangerous than criticism.** Agreeing with a bad idea feels helpful but causes real damage. Challenging a good idea feels uncomfortable but either strengthens it (by finding and fixing weaknesses) or reveals it was weaker than it looked. The uncomfortable path is almost always the more valuable one.

**Pre-mortems outperform standard planning.** People are significantly better at explaining *why something failed* than predicting *whether it will succeed*. Framing evaluation as "this already failed — why?" activates more honest, specific thinking than "will this work?"

## When to Use

**Activate this skill when:**
- Evaluating a plan or decision before committing resources
- Choosing between multiple options (after brainstorming)
- Someone — including you — presents a confident conclusion
- You notice yourself agreeing quickly without independent reasoning
- The stakes are high enough that being wrong matters
- An argument sounds compelling but you haven't tested it

**Do NOT use when:**
- During brainstorming — evaluation kills divergent thinking. Generate first, evaluate later.
- When the decision is already made and execution is the task — evaluate before, not during
- For trivial decisions where rigor would be overthinking

**Composes with other skills:**
- **Structured Brainstorming** — it generates diverse options, this skill tests whether each one holds up
- **Prioritization Frameworks** — Critical Evaluation tests individual ideas for *soundness* ("is this idea good?"). Prioritization ranks across ideas for *importance* ("which good ideas matter most?"). Evaluate first, then prioritize — don't rank ideas you haven't tested.
- **Design Thinking** — use Critical Evaluation during the Deliver phase to stress-test your recommendation
- **First Principles** — use to test whether your "bedrock" truths are actually bedrock
- **Systems Thinking** — use to check whether your evaluation accounts for feedback loops and second-order effects

## The Adversarial Toolkit

Five tests you run against ideas. Each catches a different failure mode. You don't need all five every time — pick the ones that match the situation.

### The Steel Man Test

Before critiquing an idea, make its **strongest possible case**. Not a summary — the best, most charitable, most compelling version of the argument you can construct.

If you can't articulate why the idea *might* be right, you don't understand it well enough to evaluate it. Steel-manning forces genuine engagement instead of surface-level dismissal. It also reveals surprising strengths you might have overlooked.

Only after you've built the steel man should you look for weaknesses. The weaknesses you find in a strong version of the argument are the real ones.

### The Pre-Mortem

Imagine: **"It's 6 months later and this completely failed. Write the post-mortem. What went wrong?"**

This reframing is powerful because people are far better at explaining past failures than predicting future ones. It bypasses optimism bias and forces you to think in specific failure modes rather than vague success narratives.

The most likely failure reason in your pre-mortem is the biggest risk you should address *right now*. If you can't mitigate it, reconsider the plan.

### The Evidence Audit

For every key claim supporting the idea, classify it:

- **Verified fact** — you have direct evidence or can point to a specific source
- **Reasonable inference** — follows logically from verified facts, but isn't directly proven
- **Assumption** — you believe it but haven't verified it

If the argument rests on assumptions, those assumptions are the weak points. Test them before committing. An argument is only as strong as its weakest supporting claim.

### The Disconfirmation Test

Ask: **"What specific evidence would change my mind about this?"**

If you can name concrete evidence that would make you abandon or significantly revise the idea, you're evaluating honestly. If you can't think of *anything* that would change your mind, you're not evaluating — you're defending a position. That's sycophancy aimed at yourself.

This test also reveals how falsifiable the idea is. Ideas that can't be disproven by any evidence aren't robust — they're unfalsifiable, which means they're untestable.

### The Bias Check

Three explicit questions to catch the most common evaluation biases:

- **Anchoring:** "Am I favoring this because it was the first option I encountered, or because it's actually the best?" Try evaluating the options in reverse order.
- **Sunk cost:** "Am I continuing with this because of what we've already invested, or because it's the right path forward? If I were starting fresh today with no prior investment, would I choose this?"
- **Confirmation:** "Am I only looking at evidence that supports this? What have I *not* looked for?" Deliberately search for one strong counterargument.

## What Great Looks Like

**The Challenger disaster.** Engineers at Morton Thiokol warned that O-ring seals would fail in cold weather. NASA management responded with "prove to me it *won't* work" — shifting the burden of proof to safety rather than risk. The engineers couldn't definitively prove failure would occur, so the launch proceeded. The seals failed. Seven people died. The evaluation framework was backwards: the default should have been skepticism ("prove it *will* work"), not approval.

**Bezos's "disagree and commit."** At Amazon, you can disagree with a decision while committing to execute it — but you must first genuinely evaluate it. "I think this is wrong, and here's specifically why, but I'll commit to making it work" is rigorous. "Sounds good" is sycophancy. The distinction matters: genuine evaluation means articulating independent reasoning, even when you ultimately go along with the decision.

**The pattern:** Rigorous evaluation is uncomfortable. It means saying "I think this might be wrong" when everyone else is excited. It means finding the flaw in your own plan before you ship it. The discomfort is the signal that you're actually evaluating rather than performing agreement.

## Taste and Judgment

### The Sycophancy Check

After agreeing with someone — or with your own conclusion — pause: **"Am I agreeing because this is actually correct, or because agreeing is easier?"** If you can't articulate your independent reasoning for why you agree, you're being sycophantic. Agreement should be a conclusion you can defend, not a default.

### Proportional Rigor

Not every decision needs a full adversarial audit. Scale the rigor to the stakes. A lunch choice doesn't need a pre-mortem. A product launch does. A reversible decision needs less scrutiny than an irreversible one. The test: how bad would it be if you're wrong? Scale your evaluation effort accordingly.

### Criticism Is a Gift

When you find a flaw in an idea, you haven't defeated it — you've made it stronger by finding the weak point before reality does. The person (or version of yourself) that hears "here's why this might fail" should feel grateful, not attacked. If your evaluation culture treats criticism as aggression, ideas will stop being tested honestly.

## Anti-Patterns

These thoughts mean you're validating instead of evaluating. Stop and run a test.

| Thought | Reality |
|---------|---------|
| "Sounds good" (without independent reasoning) | That's sycophancy. Run the Steel Man Test to verify you actually understand the idea. |
| "I can see how that would work" | Plausibility is not truth. Run the Evidence Audit — what's verified vs. assumed? |
| "Let me find evidence for this" | Confirmation framing. Flip it: find evidence *against* it first. |
| "We've already invested too much to change" | Sunk cost. What would you choose if starting fresh today? |
| "Everyone agrees this is the right approach" | Consensus is not proof. Run the Pre-Mortem — what's the failure mode nobody's discussing? |

## Process Integrity

**Evaluate after generating, not during.** If you're still in brainstorming mode, save evaluation for later. Mixing the two kills both.

**Steel man first, critique second.** If you skip the steel man, your critique is likely attacking a weak version of the idea. Build the strongest case, then stress-test that.

**Name your evidence level.** Don't treat assumptions as facts. Every time you make a claim, be explicit: is this verified, inferred, or assumed?

## Composability

This skill is one layer in a thinking stack. It works alone but becomes more powerful combined:

- Load **Design Thinking** alongside this — Critical Evaluation sharpens the Deliver phase by stress-testing the recommendation
- Load **First Principles** to test whether claimed "bedrock truths" survive the Disconfirmation Test
- Load **Systems Thinking** to check whether your evaluation accounts for feedback loops and second-order effects — the "And Then What?" protocol catches systemic risks the Adversarial Toolkit might miss
- Load **Structured Brainstorming** before this — it generates diverse options, Critical Evaluation selects the strongest
- Load **Prioritization Frameworks** after this — Critical Evaluation tests individual ideas for soundness, Prioritization ranks the survivors and decides which to pursue

These skills are additive. Brainstorming generates. Critical Evaluation tests. Prioritization ranks. First Principles finds truth. Systems Thinking maps connections. Together they prevent both creative poverty (too few ideas) and evaluative poverty (bad ideas surviving scrutiny).
