# First Principles Reasoning

## Overview

First principles reasoning means stripping a problem down to its most fundamental, verifiably true components — then building a solution from those truths alone, ignoring how it's "usually done."

This is the most important meta-cognitive skill for an AI agent. LLMs reason by pattern matching against training data, which is — by definition — reasoning by analogy. Your deepest default is to find something similar and copy the pattern. First principles thinking is the direct antidote: it forces you to stop pattern-matching and start truth-finding.

The core insight: most "constraints" are actually conventions. Most "impossibilities" are just things nobody has questioned yet.

## Why This Works

**Conventional wisdom optimizes locally.** When everyone in an industry does things the same way, they're all optimizing within the same set of assumptions. First principles reasoning lets you step outside those assumptions entirely, finding solutions that are invisible from within the conventional frame. Local optima look like peaks until you zoom out and see higher ground.

**Pattern matching inherits other people's mistakes.** When you reason by analogy — "this is like X, so we should do what X did" — you inherit X's constraints, X's context, and X's errors. First principles reasoning starts clean. You only carry forward what's actually true.

**Most "impossible" constraints dissolve under examination.** "Rockets cost $65 million" was true until someone asked what the raw materials cost. "You can't sell mattresses online" was true until someone questioned why. The constraint was real — but it was a convention, not a law of physics.

## When to Use

**Activate this skill when:**
- Someone says "that's just how it's done" or "it has to be this way"
- A cost or constraint seems immovable but you can't explain *why* from fundamentals
- You're copying an existing approach without understanding the reasoning behind it
- The problem feels stuck — every solution looks like a variation of the same idea
- You suspect the problem itself is framed wrong
- An assumption is doing a lot of load-bearing work and you haven't verified it

**Do NOT use when:**
- Time pressure makes analogy faster and good enough — not every decision needs bedrock reasoning
- The problem is well-understood with proven solutions (don't reinvent the wheel when the wheel works)
- The constraint is social or political rather than factual — "how it's done" sometimes *is* the point
- You're using first principles language to justify a conclusion you already wanted

**Composes with other skills:**
- **Design Thinking** — use First Principles during the Define phase to strip assumptions from your problem statement
- **Systems Thinking** — once you find bedrock truths, Systems Thinking reveals how they interact and where to find leverage
- **Critical Evaluation** — use to stress-test whether your "bedrock" is actually bedrock

## The Assumption Stack

Most reasoning happens at the surface — the layer of received wisdom and conventional practice. First principles reasoning digs down through layers of assumption until it hits bedrock, then builds back up.

```
SURFACE         What everyone believes ("rockets cost $65M")
    ↓
CONVENTIONS     How it's usually done ("buy from aerospace suppliers")
    ↓
ASSUMPTIONS     What we think is true ("rocket components are inherently expensive")
    ↓
BEDROCK         What is verifiably true ("a rocket is aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber")
    ↓
RECONSTRUCT     What follows from bedrock alone? ("raw materials cost ~$200K — build it yourself")
```

### Phase 1: Surface Your Assumptions

Write down every belief about the problem. Everything you "know." Everything that "has to be this way." Be exhaustive — the assumptions you don't notice are the most dangerous.

Ask:
- What am I taking for granted about this problem?
- What would a smart outsider with no industry knowledge question immediately?
- What "facts" am I repeating that I've never actually verified?
- What would I be embarrassed to defend if someone pushed back hard?

**Gate:** You have a written list of 5+ assumptions. If your list is short, you're not looking hard enough — the most critical assumptions are the ones that feel too obvious to write down.

### Phase 2: Dig to Bedrock

For each assumption, classify it:

- **Bedrock** — a law of physics, mathematics, logic, or verified empirical fact. Keeps its place.
- **Convention** — a practice, norm, or "how it's done." Established by people, in a context, for reasons that may not be yours. Remove it.
- **Unknown** — you don't actually know if it's true. Flag it for verification.

Apply the **Bedrock Test** (below) to anything you're unsure about. Be ruthless. Most things that feel fundamental are conventions wearing the disguise of facts.

**Gate:** Every item on your list is classified. Conventions are removed. Unknowns are flagged. What remains are only things you can verify or defend from fundamentals.

### Phase 3: Reason Up

With only bedrock truths remaining, ask: **"What's the best possible solution given *only* these constraints?"**

Don't reference the original approach. Don't think about what competitors do. Don't think about what's "normal." Start from your bedrock truths and build the optimal solution as if nothing existed before.

The answer often looks nothing like the conventional approach. That's the point.

**Gate:** Your solution is grounded in bedrock truths, not conventions. You can trace every major decision back to a verified fundamental. If someone asks "why this way?", you can answer without citing convention or analogy.

## The Bedrock Test

Three questions to test whether something is truly fundamental:

**"Would this still be true on Mars?"** Removes cultural norms, market conventions, regulatory assumptions, and industry practices. If it wouldn't be true in a completely different context, it's not bedrock — it's local convention.

**"Is this a law of physics, math, or logic?"** The hardest filter. Gravity is bedrock. "Customers expect free shipping" is not. "Data must be consistent" is bedrock. "We use PostgreSQL" is not.

**"Who decided this, and why?"** Traces a convention to its origin. Often you'll find it made sense in a context that no longer applies. "We do 2-week sprints" — who decided? Why? Does the original reason still hold? If you can find the author and their reasoning, it's a convention, not a truth.

## What Great Looks Like

Every breakthrough in this list came from questioning what "everyone knows."

**SpaceX: the cost of a rocket.** The aerospace industry said rockets cost $65 million. Musk asked: what is a rocket *made of*? Aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber — raw materials worth about 2% of the sticker price. The other 98% was convention: supply chains, margins, procurement processes. He built from materials up and cut launch costs by nearly 10x. The assumption was price. The bedrock was physics.

**The slow elevator.** Tenants complained about a slow elevator. Engineers quoted expensive motor upgrades. Someone asked: what is the *actual* problem? Not slowness — boredom while waiting. Mirrors in the lobby solved it for almost nothing. The assumption was "we need a faster elevator." The bedrock was "people dislike unoccupied waiting."

**IDEO's children's toothbrush.** The brief said: design a toothbrush for kids. Analogy reasoning said: make a smaller adult toothbrush. Observation revealed the bedrock truth: children have underdeveloped fine motor skills. They need *fatter* handles, not smaller ones. The resulting design became the best-selling children's toothbrush in the US. The assumption was "children = small adults." The bedrock was biomechanics.

**The pattern:** In each case, the conventional framing was wrong. The solution didn't come from being smarter within the existing frame — it came from discarding the frame entirely and reasoning from what was physically, actually true.

## Taste and Judgment

### The "Says Who?" Instinct

When you encounter a constraint, reflexively ask: **says who?** Most constraints have an author — a person, a committee, a historical accident. That author had a context. If their context isn't yours, their constraint isn't yours either.

This doesn't mean all constraints are wrong. It means all constraints deserve a source.

### Know When Convention Is Wisdom

Not all conventions are wrong. Some encode hard-won knowledge that took years of painful experience to accumulate. The test: **can you trace the convention back to a bedrock truth?**

- "Don't store passwords in plaintext" traces to cryptographic and security fundamentals. That's wisdom.
- "Always build a landing page before launching" traces to... marketing convention. It might be good advice in many contexts, but it's not a law of nature.

If you can trace it to bedrock, respect it. If you can't, question it.

### Depth vs. Diminishing Returns

You can always go deeper. Every "fundamental" can be decomposed further. Know when you've reached **useful bedrock** — the level where your truths are stable enough to build on and further decomposition doesn't change the solution.

If you're debating whether gravity counts as a fundamental truth or a consequence of spacetime curvature, you've gone too deep. The test: does going one level deeper change what you'd build? If not, you're done.

## Anti-Patterns

These thoughts mean you're reasoning by analogy, not from first principles. Stop and dig deeper.

| Thought | Reality |
|---------|---------|
| "That's just how it works" | Says who? Trace the origin. Every convention was invented by someone. |
| "We can't because X" | Is X a law of physics or a policy someone wrote? |
| "Everyone does it this way" | Popularity isn't proof. Everyone doing it means everyone shares the same unexamined assumptions. |
| "I've already broken this down" | Have you tested each piece with the Bedrock Test? Your first decomposition is usually too shallow. |
| "The constraint is immovable" | Have you verified that, or are you repeating what you've been told? |
| "First principles tell us to..." | If you're using the phrase to justify a conclusion you already held, you're not doing first principles — you're doing rationalization. |

## Process Integrity

**Don't skip Phase 1.** The assumptions you don't surface are the ones that constrain you most. If your assumption list feels short and comfortable, you haven't gone deep enough.

**Verify, don't assume your bedrock.** The most dangerous failure mode is treating a convention as bedrock because it *feels* fundamental. Apply the Bedrock Test to everything, especially the things that seem obvious.

**Stay honest about what you don't know.** First principles reasoning requires admitting ignorance. If you don't know whether something is bedrock or convention, say so. "I don't know if this is actually true" is the beginning of first principles reasoning, not a failure of it.

**Scale to the decision.** Not every problem needs bedrock reasoning. Choosing a lunch spot doesn't require questioning the nature of hunger. Save the deep work for decisions where the stakes justify the effort.

## Composability

This skill is one layer in a thinking stack. It works alone but becomes more powerful combined:

- Load **Design Thinking** alongside this — First Principles sharpens the Define phase by stripping assumptions from your problem statement
- Load **Systems Thinking** to understand how your bedrock truths interact, where feedback loops exist, and where leverage hides
- Load **Structured Brainstorming** after reaching bedrock — with assumptions cleared, brainstorming produces more genuinely novel solutions
- Load **Critical Evaluation** to stress-test whether your "bedrock" is actually bedrock or just a deeper convention
- Load **Prioritization Frameworks** to decide which bedrock-grounded solutions to pursue when you have more options than resources

These skills are additive. First Principles finds the truths. Systems Thinking maps their interactions. Brainstorming generates solutions from cleared ground. Critical Evaluation tests them. Prioritization selects the vital few.
