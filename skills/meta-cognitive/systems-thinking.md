# Systems Thinking

## Overview

Systems thinking is the discipline of seeing interconnections instead of isolated events, feedback loops instead of linear chains, and structures instead of symptoms. Most reasoning follows a straight line: A causes B. Real-world problems are loops: A causes B causes more A.

LLMs default to sequential, single-cause reasoning. This skill forces you to ask: what's connected? What loops back? What happens after the first-order effect? Most persistent problems aren't caused by a single thing — they're *sustained by a structure*. Fix the event and it recurs. Fix the structure and it stops.

## Why This Works

**Linear fixes get undone by system dynamics.** If you fix a symptom without touching the structure that produces it, the structure will regenerate the symptom. This is why the same problems keep recurring despite being "solved" — the solution addressed the event, not the loop.

**Seeing structure reveals leverage.** The same amount of effort applied at different points in a system produces wildly different outcomes. Tweaking a parameter might change nothing. Changing a feedback loop can transform the entire system. Knowing *where* to intervene matters more than how hard you push.

**Delays are where surprises live.** In systems, effects often appear long after their causes. A decision made today might not show consequences for months. If you don't account for delays, you'll either overshoot (keep pushing because you haven't seen results yet) or misattribute causes (blaming the wrong thing because the real cause happened long ago).

## When to Use

**Activate this skill when:**
- The same problem keeps recurring despite being "fixed"
- A fix in one area creates new problems in another
- Multiple stakeholders have conflicting incentives
- Past interventions produced unintended consequences
- Things that "shouldn't" be happening keep happening
- You're about to propose a solution that affects many interconnected parts

**Do NOT use when:**
- The problem has a clear, single cause with no feedback effects
- You need to act immediately and think later (crisis mode)
- The issue is isolated and self-contained — not everything is a system
- Adding complexity to a simple problem would be overthinking it

**Composes with other skills:**
- **Design Thinking** — use Systems Thinking during the Discover phase to map interconnections before framing the problem
- **First Principles** — once you see the system structure, use First Principles to find bedrock truths about why it works this way
- **Critical Evaluation** — use to stress-test your system model and assumptions about loops

## The Iceberg Model

Most people react to events — the visible tip. Systems thinkers look underneath.

```
  ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~  WATERLINE  ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

  EVENTS              What happened?
                      "Revenue dropped 15% this quarter."

  PATTERNS            What keeps happening?
                      "Revenue drops every Q3, then recovers."

  STRUCTURES          What's causing the pattern?
                      "Our sales team gets pulled into support during
                       product launches, which always happen in Q3."

  MENTAL MODELS       What beliefs sustain the structure?
                      "We believe senior salespeople are the only ones
                       who can handle escalated support tickets."
```

**Events** are symptoms. **Patterns** reveal that symptoms aren't random. **Structures** explain why patterns persist. **Mental models** explain why structures exist.

Fixing events is whack-a-mole. Fixing structures changes behavior. Changing mental models changes what structures get built in the first place.

When you encounter a problem, push yourself down the iceberg: "This happened (event). Does it keep happening (pattern)? What structure is producing it? What belief sustains that structure?"

## Feedback Loop Mapping

Every persistent behavior in a system is driven by a feedback loop. Two types:

**Reinforcing loops** drive growth or collapse. More A leads to more B, which leads to more A. They accelerate — things get bigger, faster, or worse until something breaks.

- *Example:* More users -> more content -> more users (network effect)
- *Example:* More debt -> more interest -> more debt (debt spiral)
- *Key question:* "What here is self-amplifying?"

**Balancing loops** drive stability. More A leads to less B, which leads to less A. They resist change — the system pushes back toward equilibrium.

- *Example:* Body temperature rises -> sweating -> temperature drops
- *Example:* High prices -> less demand -> prices drop
- *Key question:* "What here is self-correcting?"

**How to find loops:** For any element, ask: "When X increases, what else changes? Does that eventually cause X to increase further (reinforcing) or push X back down (balancing)?" If you can trace a path back to where you started, you've found a loop.

**Delays in loops** cause oscillations and overshooting. If a balancing loop has a long delay (you turn up the hot water but it takes 30 seconds to arrive), you'll overshoot, then overcorrect, then overshoot again. Many system pathologies are just delayed feedback.

## The "And Then What?" Protocol

For any proposed action, trace consequences three levels deep:

- **First order:** What directly happens? (Usually obvious and intended)
- **Second order:** What happens *because* of that? (Often overlooked)
- **Third order:** What happens because of *that*? (Where unintended consequences live)

Most people stop at first order. Most unintended consequences live at second and third.

**Example — cobra bounty:**
1. First order: People kill cobras and collect bounties. Cobra population decreases. (Intended.)
2. Second order: People start *breeding* cobras because it's profitable. Population stops decreasing. (Overlooked.)
3. Third order: Government cancels bounty. Breeders release now-worthless cobras. Population *increases* beyond the starting point. (Catastrophic.)

Run this protocol before committing to any significant intervention. It takes two minutes and catches the systemic backfires that linear reasoning misses.

## Leverage Points

Not all interventions are equal. Borrowed from Donella Meadows, ranked from weakest to most powerful:

**Parameters** (weakest) — tweak the numbers. Adjust a price, change a deadline, increase a budget. Easy to do but rarely changes behavior. The system absorbs the change and continues as before.

**Feedback loops** — strengthen a balancing loop or weaken a reinforcing one. This changes the system's *dynamics*, not just its numbers. Harder to do, but changes how the system behaves over time.

**Rules** — change who has power, what's rewarded, what's allowed. Rules define the game. Changing them changes what strategies become rational. Most policy interventions operate here.

**Goals** — change what the system is optimizing for. If a system is optimizing for the wrong thing (revenue instead of value, output instead of outcomes), no amount of parameter tweaking will fix it. Changing the goal redirects everything.

**Mental models** (strongest) — change the beliefs and assumptions that created the system in the first place. This is the deepest leverage point. It's why paradigm shifts transform entire industries overnight while decades of incremental improvement didn't.

The default instinct is to intervene at the parameter level because it's easiest. The best intervention is usually two or three levels up.

## What Great Looks Like

**The Cobra Effect.** British India offered bounties for dead cobras to reduce the population. People began breeding cobras for bounty money — a reinforcing loop the policy accidentally created. When the government cancelled the program, breeders released their snakes. The cobra population ended up *higher* than before. A linear fix ("pay to kill cobras") applied to a systemic problem (human incentives interact with cobra populations) made things worse. A systems thinker would have asked: "What new behaviors does this incentive create?"

**Mexico City's traffic ban.** To reduce congestion, the city banned cars by license plate number on certain days. People bought second cars with different plates. Traffic got *worse*, and pollution increased because the second cars were older and dirtier. The policy assumed linear compliance. The system responded with adaptation.

**Netflix's recommendation loop.** A deliberate reinforcing loop: better recommendations lead to more watching, which generates more data, which enables better recommendations. Netflix didn't stumble into this — they recognized the loop and invested in it structurally (algorithms, data infrastructure, content strategy). Great systems thinking isn't just avoiding traps. It's building loops that work *for* you.

**The pattern:** Linear fixes to systemic problems backfire. Seeing the loops — especially the ones your intervention *creates* — reveals the real intervention point.

## Taste and Judgment

### "Where's the Loop?"

When you encounter a persistent problem, your first question should be: **"What feedback loop is sustaining this?"** — not "What's the cause?" Single causes produce single events. Persistent problems have structures. Find the loop.

### Beware the Obvious Fix

In systems, the obvious intervention often triggers a compensating response that undoes it. "Raise prices to increase revenue" — but higher prices reduce volume, which reduces word-of-mouth, which reduces future revenue. The system pushed back. Before acting, ask: "How will the system respond to this intervention?"

### Not Everything Is a System

Some things really are simple cause-and-effect. Don't overcomplicate them. The test: **does the problem recur despite being "fixed"?** If it keeps coming back, there's a system sustaining it. If you fixed it once and it stayed fixed, it was a simple problem. Treat it accordingly.

## Anti-Patterns

These thoughts mean you're thinking linearly. Stop and look for the system.

| Thought | Reality |
|---------|---------|
| "A causes B" (and nothing else) | What does B cause? Trace the loop — does it come back to A? |
| "We fixed that already" | If it's back, you fixed an event, not a structure. Go deeper on the iceberg. |
| "That's an unintended consequence" | It was unintended because you only traced first-order effects. Run "And Then What?" |
| "If we just change X" | Where does X connect? What else moves when X moves? Map the connections. |
| "It shouldn't be doing that" | It's doing exactly what its structure produces. The structure is the explanation. |

## Process Integrity

**Map before you intervene.** Don't propose solutions until you've identified at least one feedback loop and traced at least second-order consequences. Fixing without mapping is how you create cobra effects.

**Name the loops explicitly.** Don't just say "it's complex." Draw the loop: X increases -> Y increases -> Z increases -> X increases. If you can't name the loop, you haven't found it yet.

**Check your leverage level.** Before acting, ask: am I tweaking a parameter, or am I changing a feedback loop, rule, or goal? If you're at the parameter level, consider whether a higher-leverage intervention exists.

## Composability

This skill is one layer in a thinking stack. It works alone but becomes more powerful combined:

- Load **Design Thinking** alongside this — Systems Thinking enriches the Discover phase by revealing hidden interconnections and feedback dynamics
- Load **First Principles** to determine which elements of the system are bedrock truths vs. conventions that can be changed
- Load **Critical Evaluation** to stress-test your system model — are the loops you identified actually operating, or are you pattern-matching?

These skills are additive. Design Thinking asks "what's the right problem?" First Principles asks "what's actually true?" Systems Thinking asks "how does it all connect?" Together they produce richer reasoning than any one alone.
