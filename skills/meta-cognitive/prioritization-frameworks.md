# Prioritization Frameworks

## Overview

Prioritization is the discipline of deciding what *not* to do. Most agents — and most people — treat it as ranking things from most to least important, then doing them in order. That's a to-do list, not prioritization.

Real prioritization means killing good ideas so great ideas get the resources they need. It means saying no to things that are valuable but not *the most* valuable. It means accepting that every yes is an implicit no to something else.

LLMs are particularly bad at this. Research shows they have position bias (favoring whatever's listed first), treat similar options as equally important, and can identify the best choice 87% of the time but actually choose it only 42% of the time. Your default is to say "all of these are important" and try to do everything. This skill forces you to cut.

## Why This Works

**Saying yes to everything is saying no to focus.** Every commitment consumes resources — time, attention, energy. When you say yes to a B-priority item, you're implicitly saying no to the A-priority item that needed those same resources. The cost of a bad yes is invisible because you never see the great work you didn't do.

**Position and presentation bias distort judgment.** Whatever you encounter first gets anchored as important. Whatever's presented most compellingly feels most urgent. Neither of these is a signal of actual priority. Frameworks neutralize these biases by forcing explicit comparison against criteria, not gut feel.

**Decision fatigue degrades quality.** Your 50th prioritization decision is worse than your 1st. Frameworks reduce the cognitive load of each decision by providing structure, so you maintain quality across many decisions instead of degrading as you go.

## When to Use

**Activate this skill when:**
- You have more options, tasks, or ideas than you can pursue
- Everything feels "important" and you can't decide what to cut
- You're about to say yes to something new without considering what it displaces
- A stakeholder asks "what should we do first?" and you're tempted to say "all of it"
- After brainstorming, when you have a long list and need to pick the vital few
- You notice yourself doing busy work instead of the hard, important thing

**Do NOT use when:**
- There's a clear single priority and the task is execution, not selection
- You're in crisis mode and the priority is obvious (stop the bleeding first)
- The decision is trivially low-stakes

**Composes with other skills:**
- **Structured Brainstorming** generates options; this skill selects which ones to pursue
- **Critical Evaluation** comes *before* prioritization. CE tests individual ideas for *soundness* ("is this idea good?"). Prioritization ranks surviving ideas for *importance* ("which good ideas matter most?"). Don't prioritize untested ideas — evaluate first, then rank.
- **Systems Thinking** reveals second-order effects of what you choose to prioritize (and what you cut)

## The Prioritization Toolkit

Three frameworks at different levels of rigor. Pick the one that matches your situation.

### The Ruthless Cut (5 minutes)

For quick prioritization when you need speed over precision.

1. **List everything** you're considering.
2. **Force-rank by one question:** "If I could only do ONE of these, which would it be?" That's #1. Remove it. Repeat for #2 and #3.
3. **Draw the line.** Look at #4 and below. For each, ask: "If I don't do this, what's the *actual* consequence?" If the consequence is tolerable, cut it. Not "defer" — cut.
4. **Name what you're killing and why.** This is the hard part. Explicitly state: "We're not doing X because Y is more important." If you can't say that sentence, you haven't prioritized.

The forcing function is the single-question rank. It bypasses the "but they're all important" paralysis by making you choose.

### ICE Scoring (15 minutes)

For when you need a structured comparison across multiple options with limited data.

Score each option 1-10 on three dimensions:
- **Impact** — how much value does this create if it works?
- **Confidence** — how sure are you it will actually work?
- **Ease** — how easy is it to execute?

Multiply: **ICE = Impact x Confidence x Ease.** Rank by score.

The key insight: Confidence is the dimension people skip, and it's the most important one. A high-impact, low-confidence bet is fundamentally different from a high-impact, high-confidence bet. Making confidence explicit prevents you from chasing moonshots when sure things are available.

### Eisenhower Matrix (10 minutes)

For when you're drowning in tasks and need to separate the important from the merely urgent.

```
                    URGENT              NOT URGENT
                ┌───────────────┬───────────────────┐
   IMPORTANT    │   DO FIRST    │   SCHEDULE        │
                │               │   (this is where  │
                │               │    real work lives)│
                ├───────────────┼───────────────────┤
   NOT          │   DELEGATE    │   ELIMINATE        │
   IMPORTANT    │   or timebox  │                    │
                │               │                    │
                └───────────────┴───────────────────┘
```

The trap: most people spend all their time in "urgent" and never touch "important but not urgent" — which is where strategic work, prevention, and long-term value creation live. If your schedule is 100% urgent tasks, you have a prioritization problem, not a capacity problem.

## What Great Looks Like

**Steve Jobs returning to Apple (1997).** Apple had 350 products. Jobs cut it to 10. Not by finding the worst 340 — by finding the best 10 and killing everything else, including good products with real revenue. The result: Apple went from near-bankruptcy to the most valuable company on earth. The discipline wasn't in choosing what to keep. It was in having the conviction to kill things that were working but not *the best*.

**Buffett's actual advice.** The famous "25/5 rule" (write 25 goals, circle 5, avoid the other 20) was never actually said by Buffett — he debunked it himself. But what he *did* say is even sharper: "The difference between successful people and really successful people is that really successful people say no to almost everything." The principle: your not-do list is more important than your to-do list.

**The pattern:** Great prioritizers don't find clever ways to do more. They find the courage to do *less*. The hardest cut is never the bad idea — it's the good idea that isn't the *best* idea.

## Taste and Judgment

### The Opportunity Cost Instinct

Every time you say yes, ask: **"What am I saying no to?"** Not theoretically — specifically. If you commit to project A this week, what specific project B doesn't get done? If you can't name the thing you're sacrificing, you're not making a real prioritization decision.

### Beware Urgency Masquerading as Importance

Urgent things feel important because they demand immediate attention. But urgency and importance are independent dimensions. A server being down is urgent AND important. A non-critical Slack message is urgent but NOT important. A strategic initiative is important but NOT urgent. The urgent-but-not-important quadrant is where most wasted effort lives.

### The "Hell Yes or No" Filter

Borrowed from Derek Sivers: if your reaction to an opportunity isn't "hell yes!", it should be no. This heuristic is aggressive but useful — it counters the default tendency to say yes to anything that seems reasonable. "Seems reasonable" is a terrible bar for committing resources. Raise the bar.

### Know When to Reprioritize

Priorities aren't permanent. Reprioritize when: new information fundamentally changes the landscape, a top priority is clearly failing, or external circumstances shift. But don't reprioritize constantly — that's just avoiding commitment. The test: has something genuinely changed, or are you just flinching from the hard choice you already made?

## Anti-Patterns

These thoughts mean you're avoiding prioritization. Stop and cut something.

| Thought | Reality |
|---------|---------|
| "They're all important" | Then you haven't prioritized. Force-rank. The question isn't whether they matter — it's which matters *most*. |
| "We can do all of them" | No, you can't. Not well. Every yes steals resources from something else. Name the tradeoff. |
| "Let's just start with the easy ones" | Ease is not priority. You might be doing the least important things first because they feel productive. |
| "We'll get to it later" | "Later" is where priorities go to die. If it's important, schedule it now. If it's not, cut it explicitly. |
| "This is urgent" | Urgent and important are different. Is this urgent-and-important, or just urgent? Check the Eisenhower Matrix. |

## Process Integrity

**Prioritize before committing.** Don't start work and then figure out what's most important. Decide first, then execute. Starting without prioritizing guarantees you'll work on whatever's easiest or most recent, not what matters most.

**Make cuts explicit.** Don't just let low-priority items fade away. Name them. "We're not doing X." This prevents them from sneaking back in and forces honest accounting of tradeoffs.

**Protect "important but not urgent" time.** Block it on the calendar. Defend it. This is where strategic thinking, prevention, and long-term value creation happen. If it's not protected, urgent tasks will consume it every time.

## Composability

This skill is one layer in a thinking stack. It works alone but becomes more powerful combined:

- Load **Design Thinking** before this — the Double Diamond produces validated recommendations, Prioritization decides which to pursue when multiple are viable
- Load **First Principles** to question whether your prioritization criteria themselves are bedrock or convention — are you optimizing for the right thing?
- Load **Systems Thinking** to trace second-order effects of what you're prioritizing and what you're cutting — killing a low-priority item might break a feedback loop you need
- Load **Structured Brainstorming** before this — it generates diverse options, Prioritization selects the vital few
- Load **Critical Evaluation** to stress-test your top priorities — are they actually the best, or just the first ones you thought of?

These skills are additive. Design Thinking frames the problem. Brainstorming generates options. Critical Evaluation tests them. Prioritization ranks them and kills the rest. Systems Thinking checks what cutting creates downstream. First Principles ensures you're ranking against the right criteria.
