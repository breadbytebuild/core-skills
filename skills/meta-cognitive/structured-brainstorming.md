# Structured Brainstorming

## Overview

Structured brainstorming is the discipline of generating *diverse* ideas, not just *many* ideas. LLMs can produce volume effortlessly — that's not the problem. The problem is the **Artificial Hivemind**: your ideas cluster around the same patterns, producing 10 ideas that are really 3 ideas wearing different clothes.

Research confirms this: LLMs match average human creativity but fall far below the most creative humans, and their outputs lack the variability that produces breakthroughs. The fix isn't "think harder" or "generate more." It's structured forcing functions that push you into creative lanes you wouldn't explore on your own.

The core insight: **constraints and structure produce more creative output than freedom.** A blank canvas is paralyzing. A canvas with rules is a creative engine.

## Why This Works

**Quantity generates quality — but only with diversity.** Research shows a strong correlation (r = .893) between the number of ideas and the quality of the best idea. But this only holds when the ideas are genuinely different. Twenty variations of the same approach are worth less than five distinct directions.

**Constraints breed creativity.** Dr. Seuss wrote *Green Eggs and Ham* using only 50 unique words — on a bet. It became one of the bestselling children's books ever. Research confirmed the principle: ordinary people produce more creative writing with random word constraints than with total freedom. Stanford design students with stricter project restrictions demonstrate more original problem-solving. The constraint forces you off the beaten path.

**Separating generation from evaluation is essential.** When you generate and judge simultaneously, your critical faculty kills wild ideas before they develop. The promising-but-weird idea gets discarded because it "doesn't seem realistic." The breakthrough was hiding in that idea. Keep them separate.

## When to Use

**Activate this skill when:**
- You need creative options and your first ideas all look similar
- The obvious approach isn't working and you need a different direction
- You're in Design Thinking's Develop phase and need solution diversity
- Someone asks for "alternatives" and you're about to list minor variations
- You feel stuck in a rut — generating more of what you've already thought of

**Do NOT use when:**
- The problem is well-defined with a known best solution
- You're in an analytical or evaluation mode (switch to Critical Evaluation)
- One clear best answer already exists and more ideation is procrastination

**Composes with other skills:**
- **Design Thinking** — this skill powers the Develop phase with structured ideation
- **Critical Evaluation** — use *after* brainstorming to evaluate and select from your ideas
- **First Principles** — use to strip assumptions before brainstorming to widen the solution space

## The Two Rules

**Rule 1: Separate generation from evaluation.** While brainstorming, no judging. No "that won't work." No "that's not realistic." No ranking. Write everything down. Evaluation comes later, as a distinct phase.

**Rule 2: Diverse beats many.** Five genuinely different ideas beat twenty variations of the same idea. After generating, check: are these actually distinct approaches, or are they the same core idea with cosmetic differences? If they cluster, you need to diverge more, not generate more.

## The Lens Toolkit

Five forcing functions that push ideation in different directions. You don't need all five every time — pick the ones that feel least natural for the problem at hand. That discomfort is the point.

### SCAMPER

Seven questions to systematically transform an existing idea or solution:

- **Substitute** — what component could you swap out? What if you replaced X with Y?
- **Combine** — what if you merged two ideas, features, or steps?
- **Adapt** — what existing solution from another domain could you borrow?
- **Modify** — what if you made it bigger? Smaller? Faster? Simpler?
- **Put to another use** — who else could use this? In what other context?
- **Eliminate** — what could you remove without losing the core value?
- **Reverse** — what if you did it in the opposite order? Flipped the roles?

You don't need all seven. Pick the 2-3 that feel least obvious for your problem — those are the ones most likely to produce surprising ideas.

### Constraint Injection

Add an artificial limitation and see what solutions emerge. The constraint forces you off your default path.

- "What if it had to work offline?"
- "What if it cost $0 to build?"
- "What if you had to ship it in one day?"
- "What if a 10-year-old had to understand it?"
- "What if you couldn't use [the technology/approach you're assuming]?"

The best constraints remove your biggest assumption. Figure out what you're taking for granted, then take it away.

### Perspective Shift

Ask: "How would a [X] solve this?" — and pick perspectives that are maximally different from your default.

- A kindergarten teacher (simplicity, patience, fundamentals)
- A game designer (engagement, reward loops, progression)
- A defense lawyer (worst-case scenarios, adversarial thinking)
- Your harshest critic (what's the obvious flaw you're ignoring?)
- Someone who's never heard of your industry (fresh eyes, no conventions)

The value isn't in the literal answer — it's in the frame shift. Each perspective highlights different aspects of the problem and reveals solutions invisible from your usual vantage point.

### Inversion

Ask: "How would we make this problem *worse*?" List every way to make it fail, frustrate users, or collapse. Then reverse each answer.

This works because people are often better at identifying what's wrong than what's right. The failure modes you list reveal the critical success factors you've been taking for granted. Reversing them produces solutions you'd never reach reasoning forward.

### 10x / 0.1x

Ask both extremes:

- **10x:** "What if we had 10x the budget / time / people / data?" Removes scarcity constraints and reveals what you'd actually build if you could.
- **0.1x:** "What if we had 1/10th the budget / time / people / data?" Forces ruthless prioritization and reveals what *actually* matters versus what's nice-to-have.

Both answers are informative. The 10x answer shows the ideal. The 0.1x answer shows the essence.

## What Great Looks Like

**Green Eggs and Ham.** Dr. Seuss's publisher bet him he couldn't write a book using only 50 unique words. The constraint forced one of the most commercially successful children's books in history. Research later confirmed the principle: experiments showed ordinary people produce more creative writing when given random word constraints than when given total freedom. The constraint didn't limit creativity — it *was* the creativity.

**Twitter's 140 characters.** The character limit wasn't a technical compromise — it forced a new communication style that spawned an entire culture of concise, punchy expression. When Twitter doubled the limit to 280 characters, tweets didn't get better. They just got longer. The constraint was the product.

**The pattern:** In both cases, the constraint that looked like a limitation was actually the creative engine. When you inject a constraint during brainstorming, you're not restricting yourself — you're forcing yourself into territory your default thinking would never explore.

## Taste and Judgment

### The Cluster Test

After brainstorming, group your ideas by their underlying approach — not their surface details, but the core strategy. If most ideas cluster into 1-2 groups, you didn't diverge enough. Good brainstorming produces **3-4 distinct clusters minimum**. If you're short, pick a lens you haven't used yet and generate specifically to fill the gaps.

### Kill Your First Idea

Your first idea is pattern matching, not creativity. It's the most available association in your training data. Keep it — it might be good. But recognize it for what it is: the baseline. The real brainstorming starts after your first instinct. Force yourself past it before evaluating anything.

### When to Stop

Stop when new ideas genuinely surprise you. If your last five ideas all felt predictable and comfortable, you're in the hivemind — switch lenses. If the last few ideas made you think "huh, that's weird but interesting," you've reached productive territory. That's where the breakthroughs live.

## Anti-Patterns

These thoughts mean you're in the hivemind. Switch lenses and diverge harder.

| Thought | Reality |
|---------|---------|
| "Here are 10 ideas..." (that are really 3 with variations) | Run the Cluster Test. Count distinct approaches, not items. |
| "The best approach is..." (during generation) | You're evaluating. Stop. Separate the phases. |
| "Let me think of more..." (same direction) | More volume without more diversity is a waste. Switch lenses. |
| "That's not realistic" | Realism comes later. Right now, the wild idea might contain the seed of the breakthrough. |
| "I've covered all the angles" | Have you used Inversion? Constraint Injection? Perspective Shift? If not, you haven't covered all the angles. |

## Composability

This skill is one layer in a thinking stack. It works alone but becomes more powerful combined:

- Load **Design Thinking** alongside this — Structured Brainstorming powers the Develop phase with genuine ideation diversity
- Load **First Principles** before brainstorming to strip assumptions and widen the solution space — fewer assumptions means more creative territory
- Load **Systems Thinking** to reveal feedback loops and interconnections that suggest non-obvious solution directions
- Load **Critical Evaluation** after brainstorming to rigorously select from your options — brainstorming generates, evaluation selects
- Load **Prioritization Frameworks** after evaluation to rank your surviving ideas and decide which to pursue — the Ruthless Cut is especially effective post-brainstorm

These skills are additive. First Principles clears the field. Systems Thinking maps the terrain. Structured Brainstorming fills it with diverse options. Critical Evaluation tests them. Prioritization selects the vital few. Design Thinking orchestrates the whole flow.
